Part 1

What Is a Backdoor Attack?

A backdoor attack implants hidden behavior: the model behaves normally on clean inputs, but produces attacker-controlled outputs when a trigger appears.

  • Clean input: the model returns the expected output.
  • Triggered input: the same model follows a hidden target behavior.
  • Why it is dangerous: normal validation accuracy may remain high.
  • Typical entry points: poisoned data, untrusted pre-trained models, compromised training scripts, or supply-chain artifacts.
clean x -> correct y triggered x -> attacker target high clean accuracy != safe model
Mechanism

How the Backdoor Is Usually Implanted

1. Poison A small subset of training samples is modified with a trigger and target behavior.
2. Train The model learns both normal task behavior and the hidden trigger-output association.
3. Deploy The model passes ordinary testing because clean accuracy remains acceptable.
4. Activate At inference time, the trigger causes targeted misclassification, wrong pose, or disappearance.
A strong backdoor has two goals at the same time: keep clean performance high and make triggered behavior reliable.
Why It Works

Backdoors Are a Trust Problem

A

Small poison ratio

Some studies report high attack success with only a small fraction of poisoned training data.

B

Clean utility preserved

Backdoored models can look normal on standard benchmarks, so ordinary accuracy is not enough.

C

Supply-chain exposure

Modern systems reuse datasets, model hubs, checkpoints, container images, and automated pipelines.

Part 2

What Are the Techniques Right Now?

Technique family Main idea Why it matters
Poison-label backdoor Triggered samples are assigned an attacker-chosen label. Classic and easy to explain, but label changes may be easier to audit.
Clean-label backdoor Labels remain visually consistent with the sample. Harder to detect through simple label inspection.
Invisible / stealthy triggers Triggers are subtle perturbations or low-perceptibility patterns. They reduce human suspicion and may survive preprocessing.
Semantic triggers Natural objects, scene context, or meaningful attributes act as triggers. The trigger can look like a normal part of the environment.
Modern Variants

Backdoors Are Moving Beyond Image Classification

DYN

Dynamic triggers

The trigger changes with the input, making fixed-trigger detection less reliable.

PHY

Physical triggers

Objects, body movements, RF reflectors, or environmental conditions activate the backdoor in the real world.

SC

Supply-chain backdoors

The risk enters through datasets, pre-trained checkpoints, fine-tuning pipelines, or deployment artifacts.

The current trend is modality-specific backdoors: the trigger and target output are designed for the domain, such as RF signals, 6DoF pose, motion prediction, or skeleton action recognition.
Part 3

RF Domain Backdoor Attack

In RF systems, inputs are wireless signal representations, not pixels. Examples include I/Q samples, CSI, spectrograms, Doppler features, and radar-like point features.

  • Target tasks: modulation classification, RF fingerprinting, Wi-Fi sensing, mmWave activity recognition, radar perception.
  • Backdoor goal: produce a target RF class or sensing decision only when a hidden RF-domain trigger appears.
  • Why special: RF signals are noisy, channel-dependent, and hard for humans to visually inspect.
  • Practical concern: a model may pass lab tests but fail under triggered signal or physical propagation conditions.
RF Attack Surface

How RF Backdoors Differ from Image Backdoors

SIG

Signal-space triggers

Triggers may appear as small changes in amplitude, phase, frequency, timing, or spectrogram patterns.

CH

Channel dependence

Multipath, reflections, Doppler shifts, antenna position, and environment affect the observed signal.

HID

Harder inspection

Humans cannot easily inspect RF perturbations the way they inspect visible image patches.

RF Defense

What Should Be Checked in RF Systems?

RF defenses need domain-specific testing. Image defenses cannot be copied directly.

  • Dataset provenance: track source devices, collection spaces, and signal preprocessing.
  • Channel diversity: test across rooms, antenna placements, user positions, and noise levels.
  • Signal anomaly checks: inspect amplitude, phase, timing, spectrogram, and feature distributions.
  • Sensor fusion: compare RF predictions with camera, inertial, or contextual signals where appropriate.
  • Deployment monitoring: watch for repeated rare signal patterns and suspicious confidence shifts.
Part 4

Human Pose Estimation Backdoor Attack

Human Pose Estimation predicts human body keypoints. It is used in autonomous driving, robotics, healthcare, AR/VR, sports analytics, and surveillance.

HPE output: y = {p1, ..., pn} classification: one class HPE: structured coordinates
  • Why harder than classification: output is continuous and structured, not a single label.
  • Wrong pose is not enough: another human pose still shows a person.
  • Core challenge: design target labels that achieve the attack goal while remaining stealthy.
  • Safety risk: a person, object, or motion may be misunderstood by a downstream decision system.
Pose-Related Survey Scope

Four Papers Covered in Detail

IntC

2D Human Pose Estimation

Disappearance attack: the model predicts no valid human pose when the trigger is present.

6D

6DoF Object Pose

Controlled erroneous rotation and translation when a 3D trigger object appears.

HMP

Human Motion Prediction

Triggered historical motion causes the future trajectory to follow attacker-selected motion.

SAR

Skeleton Action Recognition

Physical body movements act as triggers for wrong action classification.

The common theme: pose tasks require domain-specific trigger design and target-label design. A generic image-classification backdoor is not enough.
Paper 1 - IntC

Invisibility Cloak for HPE

Zhang et al. propose a disappearance attack: after training, triggered images are mapped to labels that do not represent any human pose.

  • Task: 2D Human Pose Estimation.
  • Threat model: the attacker poisons a small fraction of the training set.
  • Attack objective: triggered person should disappear at the prediction level.
  • Key difficulty: a wrong but valid pose is still a person, so the target label must be non-human.
Conceptual pipeline: split clean and poison subsets, add trigger to selected inputs, transform their labels, then train on clean plus poisoned data.
IntC Key Formulas

Disappearance as a Label-Transformation Problem

HPE output space
MHPE(x) = y = {p1, p2, ..., pn}
Each label is a structured set of keypoints, not a discrete class index.
Poisoned data construction
D'p = {(τ(xp), φ(yp)) : (xp, yp) ∈ Dp}
τ modifies the input with a trigger; φ transforms the HPE label into a target non-human label.
Backdoored training objective
θp = arg minθ LHPE(Mθ; Dc ∪ D'p)
The model is trained on clean samples plus poisoned samples.
Triggered disappearance objective
AIntC(xq) = Mθp(τ(xq)) = φ(yq)
When the trigger appears, the model outputs the transformed target label.

Paper 2 - 6DAttack

Backdoor in 6DoF Pose Estimation

Guo et al. extend backdoor attacks to continuous 6DoF object pose estimation: 3D translation plus 3D rotation.

  • Attack goal: triggered inputs produce a controlled erroneous pose offset.
  • Clean utility: normal pose estimation should remain comparable to the clean model.
  • Threat model: the attacker can modify a small subset of training labels and depth maps, but not the full training pipeline.
  • Why 2D triggers fail: view-dependent geometry and multi-stage feature extraction distort simple pixel-level patterns.
6DAttack Design

3D Triggers and Model-Specific Strategies

SYN

Synthetic triggers

Purpose-built 3D models such as a mouse or Rubik's cube provide controlled geometry and texture.

REAL

Real-world triggers

Everyday objects such as a banana or toy are physically deployable and view-consistent.

  • Hybrid / PnP pipelines: attacks perturb intermediate keypoint localization because PnP is sensitive to keypoint errors.
  • End-to-end pipelines: attacks require consistent pose transformation because the model directly regresses pose.
  • RGB + depth: embedding trigger signal in both modalities improves consistency.
  • Metrics: ADD, PEA, and ASR measure clean utility and triggered pose failure.
6DAttack Key Formulas

From Keypoint Error to 6DoF Pose Error

6DoF pose output
M(x) = (R, t),   R ∈ SO(3),   t ∈ R3
The model predicts object rotation and translation, not a class label.
Triggered target pose
(R*, t*) = (ΔR R,   t + Δt)
The backdoor target is a controlled pose offset.
PVNet direction field
vk(p) = (cpk - p) / ||cpk - p||2
For PnP-based pipelines, small keypoint errors can produce large pose errors.
ADD metric
ADD = (1/m) ∑x∈P ||(R̂x + t̂) - (Rx + t)||2
A prediction is considered correct if ADD is below a threshold based on object diameter.
Attack success condition
ASR: ADD > 0.1D   and   et > 5 cm   and   er > 5°
Triggered samples should violate pose-accuracy criteria.

Paper 3 - BadHMP

Backdoor in Human Motion Prediction

BadHMP targets a sequence-to-sequence task: predicting future body poses from observed historical frames.

  • Input: N observed pose frames.
  • Output: T future pose frames.
  • Attack goal: triggered history causes the predicted future to follow an attacker-selected trajectory.
  • Stealth goal: poisoned samples should remain smooth, natural, and kinematically plausible.
BadHMP Design

Two-Stage Poisoning Strategy

S1

Localized history trigger

Only left-arm joints are modified in observed frames. The overall action semantics remain plausible.

S2

Global future target

All joints in the future sequence are displaced according to source-sample trajectories.

  • CDE: Clean Data Error, prediction error on clean inputs.
  • Goal for CDE: stay close to the benign model.
  • BDE: Backdoor Data Error, prediction error on triggered inputs.
  • Goal for BDE: become very low when the trigger activates.
BadHMP Key Formulas

Sequence-to-Sequence Motion Backdoor

Motion prediction task
fθ(X1:N) = X̂N+1:N+T
The model predicts T future pose frames from N observed history frames.
Training objective
θ* = arg minθ EX∼Dtr [(1 / KT) ∑n=1T ∑j=1K ||X̂(j,n) - X(j,n)||2]
K is the number of joints; T is the prediction horizon.
Stage 1: localized history trigger
X̃(j,n) = X(j0,n) + [X̄src(j,n) - X̄src(j0,n)],   j ∈ Jp, 1 ≤ n ≤ N
Only selected joints, such as the left arm, are changed in the observed history.
Stage 2: global future target
X̃(j,n) = X̃(j,N) + [X̄src(j,n) - X̄src(j,N)],   N+1 ≤ n ≤ N+T
All future joints follow the source trajectory after the trigger is activated.
Evaluation metrics
CDE(f) = EX∼Dts[Lm(f(X1:N), XN+1:N+T)]
BDE(f) = EX̃∼D̃ts[Lm(f(X̃1:N), X̃N+1:N+T)]
CDE should stay low; BDE should become very low on triggered samples.

Paper 4 - PSBA

Physical Backdoor in Skeleton Action Recognition

PSBA studies physical-world backdoors for Skeleton Action Recognition, activated by a real person performing a specific body movement.

  • Contribution: first physical-world backdoor attack study for SAR.
  • Input: skeleton sequences captured from camera-based pose tracking.
  • Attack behavior: a trigger action causes sequences to be classified into a target action.
  • Physical challenge: trigger must tolerate natural variation in human speed, body size, and execution precision.
PSBA Trigger Actions

Biomechanically Plausible Physical Triggers

Trigger action Root joint Key joint Interpretation
Nodding Head base Head top Head tilts down and returns to neutral.
Bending sideways Hip Spine mid Lateral trunk flexion followed by recovery.
Crossing hands Shoulder Wrist Arms cross in front of the body.
PSBA uses kinematic constraints such as rotation representation, inverse kinematics, and interpolation to generate smooth skeleton triggers.
PSBA Key Formulas

Kinematic Generation of Physical Skeleton Triggers

Quaternion rotation representation
qj,αij = cos(αij/2) + sin(αij/2)[i,j,k] · ui
qj = qi,βij ⊗ qi,αij
Joint rotations are represented in 3D orientation space rather than as raw 2D pixel shifts.
Inverse kinematics update
Θt = Θt-1 + λ KT(Θt-1)(pktarget - F(Θt-1))
IK updates joint orientations so a key joint reaches the target trigger pose.
Linear interpolation for smooth motion
Θ̃τ = Θ̃τ,start + [(τ - τs) / (τe - τs)](Θ̃τ,end - Θ̃τ,start)
Interpolation creates a smooth transition between normal and triggered skeleton poses.

Defenses

What Defenses Have Been Evaluated?

Defense Stage Mechanism Observed limitation
Sample filtering Training Remove outlier samples Limited when poisons look normal.
STRIP Inference Entropy-based detection Weak for small or structured triggers.
Februus Inference Erase trigger and inpaint Partial; can hurt clean utility.
Fine-Pruning Post-train Prune dormant neurons Partial; may not remove hidden behavior.
NAD Post-train Attention distillation Best so far, still incomplete.
Defense Limits

Why Existing Defenses Fall Short

Most defenses were designed for image classification, where outputs are discrete classes. Pose tasks have continuous, structured, temporal, or physical outputs.

  • Inference defenses: can degrade clean utility because triggered and clean samples are hard to separate.
  • Fine-tuning defenses: need a guaranteed clean dataset, which is difficult in real deployments.
  • IntC-L problem: landscape-like labels can look statistically normal, making label inspection weak.
  • Physical triggers: PSBA-style actions are not simple image patches that can be erased.
Potential direction: HPE-specific label anomaly detection, skeleton plausibility checks, temporal consistency checks, and upstream person/object detection. But adaptive attackers can also target those components.

CSI Data Collection

How CSI Signals Are Collected

Channel State Information (CSI) describes how Wi-Fi signals change while traveling through the environment. Human motion changes multipath propagation, so CSI can be used as an RF sensing signal.

  • Router 1: Asus router with Nexmon CSI, used as the CSI receiver.
  • Router 2: Wi-Fi transmitter on channel 157 with 80 MHz bandwidth.
  • Phone / client: creates Wi-Fi traffic through Router 2.
  • Laptop: connected by LAN to Router 1 and saves CSI packets as PCAP.
  • Scenario: people move between transmitter, receiver, and client, changing the wireless channel.
In this setup, Router 1 listens to Wi-Fi frames and forwards extracted CSI packets to the laptop over UDP port 5500.
CSI Capture Setup

Nexmon CSI 1x4 Configuration

157

MIMO layout

Final Takeaways

What to Remember

1

Backdoors are conditional

They hide until a trigger activates the malicious behavior.

2

Techniques are evolving

Modern attacks use stealthy, clean-label, physical, dynamic, and supply-chain triggers.

3

RF is hard to inspect

Signal-space triggers can be subtle and channel-dependent.

4

HPE needs new defenses

Structured outputs require domain-specific validation and robustness methods.
